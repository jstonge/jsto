CSS meets ecology

In the last post, I talked about philosophers of science research agenda over the last 8 decades. But why do we care about that? More importantly, why practitioners in other fields, say ecology, might care about this kind of inquiry?

The Science of science perspective

CSS can be motivated from a science of science perspective [scisci; @fortunato_science_2018]. Scisci is in a sense more concrete than CSS; it seeks to study the structure and evolution of science itself through networks and other tools from complex science. It tackles questions on career dynamics (who publish what at what age or career stage), universal laws in citation patterns, the impact of team size on the type of science being done, how scientists select what they want to work on, and so on. Of interest is how they define science:

Science can be described as a complex, self-organizing, and evolving network of scholars, projects, papers, and ideas. [@fortunato_science_2018]

Thus, a second objective of this series is to flesh out the multilayer network of scholars, papers, institutions, ideas, and models in ecology to provide useful insights to practitioners as to where particular ideas and modeling practices come from. I hope to show that this is useful to validate practitioners intuitions about their ideas, but also that it has the potential to provide new insights on how to navigate the complex sociocultural networks that surround them.

Before going any further, why ecology? During my master, I became interested in ecology for its role in the evolution of modeling in science. Ever since the 50s and 60s, ecology positioned itself has a pioneering field in the study of complex systems using models, i.e. simplified but useful and multifunctional representations/descriptions/approximations (pick one) of latent phenomena in the world. This step, to see models as a valid means to study complex systems, is a major leap in science. Before that, models weren’t seen as an end in themselves but as a temporary step towards developing physic-like theories [@morgan_models_1999, ch.3]. An illustrative passage that shows this change of mind can be found in Joan Roughgarden’s Primer of Ecological Theory, where she describes the necessary trade-off between simplicity and details in modeling:

If you’re a physicist, talks about kinds of models, and negotiations between simplicity and detail, seems strange indeed. One doesn’t negotiate about F = ma or the laws of thermodynamics. So ecology is different and it’s a good idea to accept this now. For the most part, mathematical models in ecology are not foundational, they are supplemental, and judged by their utility. [@roughgarden_primer_1998]

Something has changed with the practice of modeling in ecology. I feel that ecology made us more comfortable with the idea that an approximate representation of the world might be good enough. Echoing philosopher William C. Wimsatt, ecologists were among the first to methodologically embrace the fact that we are limited beings in a very complex world, and that models might be our best shot at representing reality [see @wimsatt_re-engineering_2007 for more on how we are limited beings]. Anyway, one key objective of this series of posts will be to better understand the coevolution of modeling practices and social structure, e.g. how socio-technical environments influence the practice of modeling, from their interpretations to the types of models we use, and, in turn, how modeling influence the fabric of social interactions when we do science.

Now, all of this will require some data.

Show me what you got

JSTOR Data for Research (DfR) is a large database that contains over 12 million academic journal articles. Among these journals, some of them are ecology-related. What ecology-related journals do JSTOR DfR have? Starting from the premise that science is a network of authors, papers, institutions, and ideas, we circumscribe the scope of our investigation to the journals published by the main ecological societies, namely Ecological Society of America, British Ecological Society, Oikos, and the American Society of Naturalists.

Additionally, we consider well-known journals such as Science, Proceedings of the National Academy of Science and The Royal Society that are known to publish ecological content. We use the category by JSTOR DfR to identify the articles within these journals that are related to ecology.

There are some journals we are unclear if we should keep or not. For example, is the Journal of Biogeography part of ecology? On their website, editors claim that the journal lies at the intersection of biology and geography. Ecology being part of biology, then the journal is partly ecology, right? Well, I am not so sure. Biology today covers many scales and different worldviews. Thus, saying that a journal is in biology does not imply that it is ecology. But we know that some famous insular biogeographers, such as Robert MacArthur, are also well-known ecologists. Instead of settling for a yes and no answer, for now we are keeping the Journal of Biogeography for experimentation. We will seek to find ways to empirically assess whether, or to what extent, a journal is or is not related to ecology.

Given the above criteria, JSTOR DfR have 17 ecology-related journals:

Our corpus contains 17 journals from two databases (Portico and JSTOR), and have very different life spans and volume of published articles. For later use, I represent the number of articles for which we have abstracts as shaded bars. We can see that most often we miss more contemporary abstracts because the Portico database doesn’t seem to have done such as good job capturing them.

Each row is an article, meaning that we have 106,850 articles in our dataset. That said, what count as an article? Do editorial, preface, or erratum count as articles? For now, we will just consider everything as an article. Anyway, we can see that for each article we have its id (provided by JSTOR), title, pub_year, abstract, publisher, jn_title, authors, wc (e.g. word count of the article), doi (extracted from the various identifiers), doc_id (our own custom id we will use as unique identifiers), and so on.

From the given word count, we calculate that our corpus is around 656 million words. We do not have directly access to all these words, though. To access the full text, one must first take an arrangement with the JSTOR DfR team. And that is exactly what I did for my master thesis. My interaction with them was nice, and within a few weeks they gave me access to over 13G of text data. Unfortunately, you get the raw data from their Optical Character Recognition (OCR) algorithm, ran over their PDFs of varying shape and quality. To be honest, to clean that up is a mess and something I will keep for a later post. This is why we begin with exploring something nice and easy in the next post, namely the social structure hidden in coauthorship networks.

On that, thanks for reading this introductory post and I will see you later!
